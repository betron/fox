Poem About My Teeth

1.

Having endured six painful years of braces
(age 11 to 16)
I can’t help but get a little pissed off
whenever people complain
of their 2 year stint with the retainer
or their 3 year dance with the dentist
or their 4 year marriage to the brackets
or their 5 year—their really very meager
5 year ordeal with the devil
a.k.a.
the orthodontist.

I mean it really gripes my ass.

This is exacerbated by the fact
that my own particular devil, turns out
wasn’t even a real devil—
just a dentist
with a course in devilry
a.k.a.
orthodontics.

And whenever he used what looked like
a cross between a hammer and a screwdriver
to push hard against a tooth, pushing
for god knows what, whenever he slipped,
when the screwhammer slid
over the saliva
and, carried by inertia
into my cheek or gum
it hurt.
A lot.

2.

So when, years later
(age 19)
my parents dragged me to some oral surgeon
to examine the progress of my wisdom teeth
we were distressed after hearing the diagnosis.
My teeth were becoming distressed
by the fact that I had 4, yes 4
impacted molars, should have come in
after puberty, said Dr. Kleine.
We were distressed by his facial expression
and his serious, bushy eyebrows
as he brought the grey screen of my Panorex into focus.

“I get about 10 cases a year this bad,” he explained,
“Your lower left molar is right on the nerve
that runs through your jaw. There’s a
15% chance
that after the surgery
you could have permanent numbness
in your cheek, mouth, or tongue.”

Stunned, I asked what would happen
if we did nothing.

“It could cause all kinds of problems—
tooth erosion, periodontal disease,
to say nothing of destroying
the teeth that are next to it,
or causing all your teeth to shift back
to the crookedness you had corrected
with braces.”

Shaking now, I said I suppose
we have to do it,
that it was just 15%
after all, right?

“Well, it’s 100%
if it happens to you,”
he said
half-jokingly
I think.

3.

We were distressed, my parents and I, back at the house
but despite all the yelling, the arguing, the crying—
I couldn’t help but find it a little funny
how my parents were trying to blame the whole thing
on Dr. Kleine, calling him “a hasty butcher.”

I pictured him in his backyard, in the summer,
sweating, mosquitoes floating around his grey hair
with a steak
or a carving board
frantically trying to do it
as fast as possible,
whatever it was that he was trying to do,
so long as it was something
that demanded rapidity,
something serious,
something very bloody.
